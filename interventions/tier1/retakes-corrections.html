What It Is

Students have opportunity to demonstrate improved mastery after initial assessment, with or without penalty.

What Quality Implementation Looks Like

‚úÖ In the Classroom (What TO Do):

  • Clear policy communicated at start of year (who, when, how)
  • Students must show evidence of learning before retake (not just guessing again)
  • Retakes assess same skills, but different questions
  • Process is efficient for teacher (not overwhelming)
  • Students take ownership (they initiate, not teacher chasing them)
  • Builds growth mindset: Learning continues after the test

‚ùå Red Flags (What NOT to Do):

  • "Retakes anytime for anyone" with no structure (unsustainable for teacher)
  • Exact same test given again (students memorize answers, don't learn)
  • No requirement to study/relearn before retake
  • Unlimited retakes (becomes gaming the system)
  • Only "good" students allowed to retake
  • Teacher resentment because policy is too burdensome

Policy Types

Policy Type Who Can Retake? Requirements Before Retake Grading Best For
Full Mastery
(Standards-Based)		 Anyone scoring below 80% Complete corrections sheet + attend review session Retake replaces original grade completely Standards-based grading; growth mindset culture
Partial Credit Anyone below 70% Complete review worksheet or corrections Average of original and retake OR retake capped at 80% Traditional grading; balances accountability and growth
Targeted Retake Anyone, but only retake questions missed Submit error analysis: "Why did I miss this?" Only missed questions replaced Efficient; focuses on gaps
Test Corrections for Partial Credit Everyone Correct errors, explain why original was wrong Earn back up to 50% of missed points Less time-intensive; still promotes learning
Project/Essay Revision Anyone who submits on time Conference with teacher + revision plan Revised grade replaces original OR limited to one letter grade improvement Major assignments; emphasizes process

Implementation Steps

Step 1: Establish Clear Policy

(Communicate Early and Often)

Sample Policy for Middle School: RETAKE POLICY - Mr. B's Social Studies WHO: Any student scoring below 80% on a quiz or test WHEN: Within 1 week of receiving graded assessment back REQUIREMENTS BEFORE RETAKE: 1. Complete Test Corrections Form (analyze errors) 2. Attend Retake Review Session (office hours or lunch) 3. Submit Retake Request Form (signed by student and parent) THE RETAKE: - Similar questions, different content - Same format (if original was multiple choice + short answer, retake will be too) - Taken during office hours, lunch, or before/after school GRADING: - Retake grade replaces original grade up to 80% - If you score below original, original grade stands - You get ONE retake per assessment EXAMPLE: Original grade: 65% After corrections and review, retake grade: 85% Final grade: 80% (capped)

Post this policy:

  • On Canvas
  • On classroom wall
  • In syllabus sent home
  • Review it before first assessment

Step 3: Require Learning Before Retake

Option 1: Retake Review Session (Most Effective)

  • Offer 2-3 times per week (e.g., Monday/Wednesday lunch)
  • Students attend with their corrections form
  • Mini-reteach of commonly missed concepts (10 min)
  • Students study/ask questions (15 min)
  • Students can take retake at end of session OR schedule for later

Option 2: Differentiated Review Assignment

Based on Test Corrections Form, assign targeted practice:

  • Missed vocabulary questions? ‚Üí Complete vocabulary review worksheet
  • Missed cause/effect questions? ‚Üí Complete cause/effect graphic organizer
  • Missed evidence-based questions? ‚Üí Practice CER responses

Students submit this review work before retake

Option 3: Student-Led Study Plan

  • Student proposes their own study plan based on errors
  • Must include: What I'll study, how I'll study it, when I'll study
  • Teacher approves plan
  • Student shows evidence of completing plan (notes, practice, etc.)

Making Retakes Logistically Manageable

For Teachers:

Batch Retakes:

  • Don't offer retakes daily (you'll be overwhelmed)
  • Set specific retake times: "Retakes available Monday lunch, Wednesday after school, Friday before school"
  • Multiple students can retake at once (different assessments)

Use Student Helpers:

  • Responsible student can proctor retake while you work with others
  • Older student aid can supervise (if available)

Digital Retakes:

  • Use Google Forms or Canvas quizzes for multiple choice
  • You review short answers
  • Reduces printing and grading time

Common Challenges & Solutions

‚ùå Challenge: "Students don't bother studying before retake"

‚úÖ Solution:

  • Make corrections form mandatory (can't retake without it)
  • Require review session attendance OR completed review assignment
  • Make retake different enough that memorizing won't work

‚ùå Challenge: "Students procrastinate and want retake after deadline"

‚úÖ Solution:

  • Strict 1-week deadline (grades close after that)
  • Natural consequence: "I'm sorry, the retake window has closed. Let's focus on the next assessment."
  • Can make exception for absence, but require documentation

‚ùå Challenge: "High achievers want to retake 89% to get 100%"

‚úÖ Solution:

  • Set threshold: Retakes only for scores below 80% (or your chosen cutoff)
  • Explain: "This policy is for students still developing mastery, not for perfectionists"
  • Offer enrichment instead: "You've shown mastery. Want to explore this topic more deeply?"

‚ùå Challenge: "Some students never pick up graded tests"

‚úÖ Solution:

  • Hand back tests in class (don't let students leave without it)
  • Call home for students who scored below threshold: "Hi, I wanted you to know [student] can retake..."
  • Post retake opportunities on Canvas

‚ùå Challenge: "I'm drowning in retakes to grade"

‚úÖ Solution:

  • Batch them (all retakes on same day)
  • Limit: One retake per assessment (not unlimited)
  • Use scantron or digital quizzes when possible
  • Consider test corrections for points instead of full retake

Student Reflection After Retake

RETAKE REFLECTION Original Score: ______ Retake Score: ______ 1. What did you do differently to prepare for the retake? 2. What studying strategies worked best for you? 3. What will you do differently for the NEXT assessment (so you don't need a retake)? 4. What did you learn about yourself as a learner?

This helps students develop metacognition and study skills.
