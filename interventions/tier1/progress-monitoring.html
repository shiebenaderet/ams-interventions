What Is It?

Progress monitoring is the regular, systematic checking of student understanding and skill development to make instructional decisions. Rather than waiting for a unit test to discover students haven't learned, progress monitoring provides ongoing feedback that allows teachers to adjust instruction in real-time.

Research Base: Progress monitoring with feedback has an effect size of 0.70+ (Hattie). When teachers use data to adjust instruction, student achievement increases significantly. This is a cornerstone of effective MTSS implementation.

What Quality Implementation Looks Like

‚úÖ In the Classroom (What TO Do):

  • Data collected frequently (weekly or bi-weekly for key skills)
  • Data is actually USED to change instruction (not just collected)
  • Students can see their own progress over time
  • Multiple data sources used (not just tests)
  • Quick turnaround on feedback (within 1-2 days)
  • Students set goals based on their data
  • Decisions are made: Who needs reteaching? Who's ready for enrichment?

‚ùå Red Flags (What NOT to Do):

  • Data collected but never analyzed or acted upon
  • Months between data collection points (can't adjust instruction)
  • Only summative assessments used (too late to help)
  • Students don't know how they're doing until report cards
  • Data collection takes up too much instructional time
  • No action taken based on data - teaching continues unchanged
  • Only tracking failing students (not monitoring all students)

Step-by-Step Implementation

Step 1: Choose What to Monitor (Start Small!)

Don't try to monitor everything. Choose 2-3 critical skills in your content area - skills that build over time, not one-time lessons.

Example for 8th Grade Social Studies: ‚Ä¢ Skill 1: Writing CER (Claim-Evidence-Reasoning) responses ‚Ä¢ Skill 2: Analyzing primary sources ‚Ä¢ Skill 3: Understanding cause and effect in history

Step 2: Create Simple, Repeatable Assessments

For each skill, create a quick check you can give repeatedly:

  • Same format each time (so growth is clear)
  • 5-10 minutes maximum
  • Easy to score (rubric or right/wrong)
Example CER Rubric (4-point scale): 4 - Clear claim, specific evidence with citation, reasoning connects evidence to claim 3 - Clear claim, evidence present, reasoning needs development 2 - Claim present, evidence missing OR reasoning missing 1 - Incomplete response

Step 3: Build Monitoring into Your Routine

Day Progress Monitoring Activity
Monday Bellwork: Analyze a primary source (track on chart)
Wednesday Exit ticket: Write claim based on day's lesson
Friday Weekly Quiz: 1 CER response + 2 multiple choice on week's content

Step 4: Track Data Visually

Option A: Class Data Tracker (Teacher Use)

  • Spreadsheet with student names and weekly scores
  • Color-code: Green (mastery), Yellow (approaching), Red (needs support)
  • Use to form small groups

Option B: Student Data Tracker (Student Use)

My CER Writing Progress Week 1: 2/4 Week 2: 2/4 Week 3: 3/4 ‚Üê I'm improving! Week 4: 3/4 Week 5: ? Week 6: ? Goal: 4/4 by end of unit

Step 5: Use Data to Make Decisions

After Each Data Collection, Ask:

  1. Who mastered it? (These students need enrichment)
  2. Who is close? (These students need targeted practice)
  3. Who is struggling? (These students need reteaching/intervention)

Then Take Action:

  • Small Group Reteach (next day, 10 min): Pull struggling students, reteach
  • Peer Tutoring: Pair mastery students with those who are close
  • Adjust Whole Class Instruction: If 70%+ struggled, reteach to everyone
Example Decision Making: Friday's quiz results: ‚Ä¢ CER Responses: 60% of students scored 2/4 or below ‚Ä¢ Decision: Monday's lesson will review evidence selection and reasoning Week 4 data shows: ‚Ä¢ 8 students consistently scoring 1-2/4 on CER responses ‚Ä¢ Decision: These 8 students get Tier 2 intervention (writing group with counselor or support teacher)

Student-Facing Progress Monitoring

Monthly Student Conference (5 minutes per student)

Teacher: "Let's look at your CER writing progress. What do you notice?" Student: "I started at 2 and now I'm at 3." Teacher: "Yes! What helped you improve?" Student: "Using the sentence frames for reasoning." Teacher: "Excellent. To get to a 4, what do you need to work on?" Student: "Finding better evidence from the text." Teacher: "Right. Let's set a goal: By next Friday's quiz, find at least 2 pieces of evidence before choosing the best one. I'll check in with you Wednesday."

Whole Class Data Discussion

Every 2-3 weeks, share class-wide progress (no individual names):

  • "70% of you are now at mastery level on CER writing - great growth!"
  • "As a class, we're struggling with finding strong evidence. Let's focus on that this week."
  • "Our goal is 80% mastery by the end of the unit. We're at 65% now."

Common Pitfalls & Solutions

‚ùå Pitfall: Data Collection Overload

Problem: Spending so much time collecting data that there's no time to teach

Solution: Use quick checks (3-5 min). Limit to 2-3 key skills. Build data collection into existing routines (exit tickets, bellwork).

‚ùå Pitfall: Data Sits Unused

Problem: Collecting data but never looking at it or changing instruction

Solution: Block 15 minutes weekly to review data and make ONE instructional decision. Start small: "Based on this data, I will..."

‚ùå Pitfall: Students Don't See Their Progress

Problem: Teacher tracks data but students don't know how they're doing

Solution: Students graph their own data. Monthly goal-setting conferences. Celebrate growth publicly (no names, just class %)

‚ùå Pitfall: Only Tracking "The Grade"

Problem: Overall grades don't show skill mastery or growth areas

Solution: Track specific skills separately. "You got a C, but you're mastering evidence selection and need work on reasoning."

Making Data Actionable

The 3-Question Decision Framework

After collecting data, ask these three questions:

1. Is my instruction working?
  • If 70%+ students show mastery ‚Üí Keep going, add enrichment
  • If 50-69% show mastery ‚Üí Review key concepts, continue practice
  • If <50% show mastery ‚Üí Reteach using different approach
2. Who needs what?
  • Mastery students ‚Üí Extension activities, leadership roles
  • Approaching students ‚Üí Targeted practice, peer tutoring
  • Struggling students ‚Üí Small group reteaching, Tier 2 consideration
3. What do I need to change?
  • Whole class instruction? (If most students struggling)
  • Small group interventions? (If subset struggling)
  • Pacing? (Too fast/slow based on mastery rate)
  • Assessment format? (Maybe students know it but can't show it this way)

Quick Start Guide

Start Progress Monitoring This Week:

Day 1:
  1. Choose ONE skill to monitor
  2. Create a 4-point rubric or simple scoring guide
Day 2:
  1. Give students a 5-minute check on that skill
  2. Score using your rubric (should take 10-15 min total)
Day 3:
  1. Enter scores in simple spreadsheet
  2. Sort by score: Who's struggling? Who's mastering?
  3. Make ONE decision based on data
Week 2:
  1. Repeat the same check
  2. Track: Did students improve?
  3. Adjust instruction based on results
