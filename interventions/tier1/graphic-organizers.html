What It Is

Visual frameworks that help students organize information, see relationships between ideas, and plan their thinking.

What Quality Implementation Looks Like

‚úÖ In the Classroom (What TO Do):

  • Teacher models filling out graphic organizer with think-aloud
  • Students practice with support before independent use
  • Same graphic organizer used repeatedly for same task (builds automaticity)
  • Organizers match the thinking task (sequence? comparison? cause-effect?)
  • Used as tool during learning, not just assessment

‚ùå Red Flags (What NOT to Do):

  • Graphic organizer given with no instruction on how to use it
  • Different organizer every time (students never master any)
  • Too complex (more time spent understanding organizer than content)
  • Only used for "struggling students" (should be universal)
  • Organizer doesn't match the task

Teaching a New Graphic Organizer

Name and Purpose (2 min)

"This is a Venn Diagram. We use it when comparing two things." Show blank organizer on board/screen

Model with Think-Aloud (5 min)

Use simple, familiar example (pizza vs. tacos, soccer vs. basketball) Teacher fills it out while talking through thinking: "Pizza has a crust, so I'll write that on the pizza side..." "Both are foods, so that goes in the middle..." Make mistakes and fix them: "Oops, both have cheese, let me move that to the middle"

Guided Practice (5 min)

  • New simple example (dogs vs. cats, winter vs. summer)
  • Students fill out organizer while teacher circulates
  • Call on students to share: "What did you put in the middle? Why?"
  • Display student examples

Partner Practice (5 min)

  • Assign content-related comparison (more challenging)
  • Partners work together
  • Share out key findings

Independent Use (next day)

  • Students use same organizer type with new content
  • Provide blank organizer + instructions
  • Remind: "Remember, this is just like when we compared dogs and cats"

Building Routine with Graphic Organizers

Option 1: Consistent Organizer for Consistent Task

  • Every time students compare/contrast ‚Üí Venn Diagram
  • Every time students write argument ‚Üí CER chart
  • Students learn: "When I see a comparison task, I grab my Venn"

Option 2: Graphic Organizer Menu

  • Post 3-5 common organizers students have mastered
  • "Choose the organizer that will help you plan this essay"
  • Builds metacognition: Which tool for which job?

Making Them Accessible

  • Digital versions on Canvas (Google Drawings templates, fillable PDFs)
  • Paper copies always available in classroom bin
  • Posted on wall as reference
  • Embedded in assignment directions: "Use the CER organizer to plan your lab conclusion"
