What Is It?

Frequent check-ins are brief, structured conversations between teacher and student(s) to monitor progress, provide encouragement, address concerns, and adjust support as needed. Unlike casual conversations, these check-ins are intentional, scheduled, and focused on specific goals - whether academic, behavioral, or social-emotional.

Research Base: Check-in/Check-out (CICO) interventions have strong research support, with effect sizes ranging from 0.60-0.80 for reducing problem behavior and improving academic engagement. Regular check-ins build relationships, increase accountability, and allow for rapid response when students struggle.

What Quality Implementation Looks Like

‚úÖ What TO Do:

  • Scheduled consistently - same time, same place (e.g., every morning at 8:05, every Friday after 4th period)
  • Brief and focused - 2-5 minutes maximum
  • Specific goals - checking on clear, defined targets (not just "how are you?")
  • Data-driven - use a simple rating system or checklist
  • Positive and encouraging - start with strengths, end with next step
  • Two-way conversation - student reflects, not just teacher talking
  • Follow-through - if student needs something, you address it
  • Record-keeping - track patterns over time

‚ùå Red Flags:

  • Check-ins happen "when there's time" (no schedule = won't happen)
  • Conversations are too long (10+ minutes, becomes unsustainable)
  • No clear purpose or goal ("Just checking in...")
  • Focus only on problems, not progress
  • Teacher does all the talking
  • No data collected (can't see if it's working)
  • Check-ins used as punishment or interrogation
  • Student doesn't know why they're being checked on

Types of Check-Ins

1. Academic Progress Check-Ins

Purpose: Monitor student progress toward specific academic goals

Example Goals:

  • Completing homework 4/5 days this week
  • Turning in assignments on time
  • Maintaining C or better in class
  • Using graphic organizers for note-taking
  • Asking for help when stuck
Teacher: "Let's look at your goal sheet. Your goal was to complete 4/5 homework assignments this week." Student: "I got 3 done." Teacher: "That's progress from last week's 2! What helped?" Student: "I started using my planner." Teacher: "Great strategy. What will help you reach 4 this week?" Student: "Maybe do homework right after school instead of waiting." Teacher: "Let's try that. Check back Friday!"

2. Behavioral Check-Ins (CICO - Check-In/Check-Out)

Purpose: Increase positive behavior, reduce problem behavior

Example Goals:

  • Following directions the first time
  • Keeping hands and feet to self
  • Using respectful language
  • Staying on task during independent work
  • Transitioning quietly

Morning Check-In (2 min):

Teacher: "Good morning! Ready for today?" Student: "Yeah." Teacher: "Your goal is following directions the first time. Let's aim for 4/5 periods green today." Student: "Okay." Teacher: "You got this. I'll check in at lunch. Have a great morning!" [Give student daily tracking sheet]

Afternoon Check-Out (3 min):

Teacher: "How'd it go today?" Student: "I got 3 greens and 2 yellows." Teacher: "Three greens! Which classes?" Student: "Math, science, and social studies." Teacher: "What helped in those classes?" Student: "I sat near the front and listened." Teacher: "Smart! Tomorrow, try that in all classes. Parent signature needed - see you tomorrow!"

3. Social-Emotional Check-Ins

Purpose: Monitor student well-being, identify when additional support is needed

Example Focus Areas:

  • Stress/anxiety levels
  • Peer relationships
  • Family situations affecting school
  • Emotional regulation
  • Connection to school
Teacher: "How are you doing this week? Rate your stress 1-10." Student: "Maybe a 7." Teacher: "That's higher than usual. What's going on?" Student: "I have three tests this week." Teacher: "That IS a lot. Let's look at your schedule. Can we move one deadline?" Student: "Maybe the project?" Teacher: "Let me check with your other teacher. In the meantime, let's make a study plan for these tests."

4. Executive Functioning Check-Ins

Purpose: Support organization, time management, planning

Example Goals:

  • Using planner consistently
  • Organizing binder weekly
  • Breaking long-term projects into steps
  • Tracking missing assignments
  • Managing time for homework
Teacher: "Let's do a quick binder check." [Student opens binder] Teacher: "I see your science notes in the English section. What's your system?" Student: "I don't really have one." Teacher: "Let's set one up. Each subject gets a section, papers go in right away. Can you reorganize this weekend?" Student: "Yeah." Teacher: "Show me Monday. If it's organized, you've met your goal!"

Step-by-Step Implementation

Phase 1: Identify Students and Set Goals (Week 1)

Step 1: Determine Who Needs Check-Ins

Use data to identify:

  • Students with multiple missing assignments (3+)
  • Students with behavior referrals (2+ in a month)
  • Students whose grades dropped suddenly
  • Students who seem disengaged or withdrawn
  • Students returning from absence (illness, suspension)
  • Students experiencing known stressors (family, social)
Start small: Choose 2-4 students maximum if you're doing individual check-ins

Step 2: Set Specific, Measurable Goals

‚ùå Not vague: ‚úÖ Specific:
"Do better in class" "Complete 4/5 homework assignments each week"
"Improve behavior" "Follow directions the first time in 4/5 classes per day"

Step 3: Create a Schedule

Pick a consistent time that works:

  • Morning: Before classes start
  • Passing period: Between specific classes
  • Lunch: Before or after
  • End of day: Before dismissal
  • Weekly: Friday advisory, Monday morning, etc.
Put it on YOUR calendar - if it's not scheduled, it won't happen

Phase 2: Running Effective Check-Ins (Weeks 2-8)

Morning Check-In Structure (2-3 minutes)

  1. Greet warmly
    "Good morning! How are you feeling today?" [Listen briefly, acknowledge]
  2. Review goal
    "Your goal today is [specific goal]. You've been doing great with this - yesterday you earned 4/5 greens."
  3. Set intention
    "What's your plan to reach your goal today?" [Student shares] "That's a good plan. I'll check in with you at 3:00 to see how it went."
  4. Give support/encouragement
    "You've got this. Remember, if you need help, just ask. Have a great day!" [High five, fist bump, or other positive send-off]

Afternoon Check-Out Structure (3-5 minutes)

  1. Review the day
    "How do you think today went?" [Student self-assesses] "Let's look at your point sheet." [Review data together]
  2. Celebrate successes
    "You earned 4/5 greens! That's awesome! What helped?" [Student identifies what worked] "Yes! I noticed in math class you raised your hand right away when you had a question."
  3. Problem-solve challenges
    "I see you got a yellow in English. What happened?" [Student explains] "What could you try differently tomorrow?" [Help student problem-solve]
  4. Plan for tomorrow
    "Tomorrow, let's aim for 5/5 greens. You're so close! See you tomorrow morning at 8:00." [Parent/guardian signature on sheet]

Fading Check-Ins (Gradual Release)

When student is consistently meeting goal:

  • Phase 1: Daily ‚Üí Every Other Day
    "You've been doing awesome! Let's try checking in Monday, Wednesday, Friday this week."
  • Phase 2: Every Other Day ‚Üí Twice Per Week
    "Great job! This week, let's check in just Monday and Friday."
  • Phase 3: Twice Per Week ‚Üí Weekly
    "You're really independent now! Let's check in just Fridays for the next few weeks."
  • Phase 4: Weekly ‚Üí As Needed
    "I don't think you need regular check-ins anymore - you've got this! But I'm always here if you need to check in. Stop by anytime."
Monitor after fading: Keep an eye on student's data. If they start to slip, re-institute check-ins temporarily.

Common Pitfalls & Solutions

‚ùå Pitfall: Check-Ins Feel Like Punishment

Problem: Student sees check-ins as "being in trouble"

‚úÖ Solution:

  • Frame positively: "I'm checking in because I know you can be successful"
  • Start and end with positives
  • Use neutral time/location
  • Celebrate successes publicly, struggles privately

‚ùå Pitfall: Check-Ins Are Inconsistent

Problem: Sometimes daily, sometimes skip a week

‚úÖ Solution:

  • Set alarm/reminder on phone
  • Block time in calendar (non-negotiable)
  • Have backup: If you're absent, who does check-in?
  • Track: Keep log of when check-ins happen

‚ùå Pitfall: Check-Ins Become Too Long

Problem: 10-15 minute conversations are unsustainable

‚úÖ Solution:

  • Use timer (set for 3 minutes)
  • Stick to structure: Review ‚Üí Celebrate ‚Üí Plan ‚Üí Done
  • If deeper conversation needed, schedule separate time
  • Practice brevity

‚ùå Pitfall: No Data = No Accountability

Problem: Just having conversations with no tracking

‚úÖ Solution:

  • Use simple tracking sheet (daily points, weekly yes/no)
  • Graph progress with student
  • Without data, you can't tell if it's working
  • Student needs to see their own progress

‚ùå Pitfall: Goals Are Too Vague

Problem: "Try harder" or "do better" - not measurable

‚úÖ Solution:

  • Make goals specific and measurable
  • "Complete 4/5 homework assignments" not "do more homework"
  • "Raise hand before speaking 8/10 times" not "participate more"
  • If you can't count it, revise the goal

‚ùå Pitfall: Teacher Does All the Talking

Problem: Check-in becomes lecture, not conversation

‚úÖ Solution:

  • Ask open-ended questions: "How do you think today went?"
  • Let student do most of the talking
  • Teacher's job: Listen, ask, guide - not lecture
  • Student needs to self-assess and problem-solve

Check-In Conversation Starters

For Academic Check-Ins:

  • "What's going well in class this week?"
  • "Where did you get stuck, and what did you try?"
  • "What's your plan for finishing this assignment?"
  • "What grade are you aiming for, and what will it take?"
  • "What's one thing I can do to help you succeed?"

For Behavioral Check-Ins:

  • "Which class went best today? What did you do differently there?"
  • "What's your goal for tomorrow?"
  • "What strategies helped you follow directions today?"
  • "If you could do one thing differently today, what would it be?"
  • "What do you need from me to be successful tomorrow?"

For Social-Emotional Check-Ins:

  • "How are you feeling today, really?"
  • "Rate your stress level 1-10. What would make it lower?"
  • "What's something good that happened this week?"
  • "Is there anything I should know about what's going on?"
  • "What's one thing you're looking forward to?"

Monitoring Success

How do you know check-ins are working?

Student-Level Indicators:

  • Student is meeting their goal more consistently over time
  • Student can self-assess their progress accurately
  • Student uses strategies discussed in check-ins
  • Student's grades, behavior, or engagement improves
  • Student seeks out check-ins (sees them as supportive, not punitive)

When It's Working:

  • You see upward trend in data over 3-4 weeks
  • Student takes ownership of goal
  • Other teachers notice improvement
  • Student no longer needs check-ins to be successful

When It's NOT Working:

  • No improvement after 4-6 weeks of consistent check-ins
  • Student's data is flat or declining
  • Student is resistant or avoidant
  • Action: Reassess goal, adjust approach, or consider Tier 3 support

Remember

Check-ins are about relationship, accountability, and responsiveness.

  • Relationship: You care enough to check in consistently
  • Accountability: Student knows someone is paying attention
  • Responsiveness: You adjust support based on what you see

When done well, check-ins are one of the most impactful Tier 2 interventions because they combine data, relationships, and timely support.

Start small, be consistent, celebrate progress!
