What Is It?

Small group instruction is targeted teaching provided to a subset of students who need additional support to master specific skills or concepts. Unlike whole-class instruction, small groups allow for more intensive teaching, immediate feedback, and instruction precisely matched to student needs. This is a cornerstone of Tier 2 intervention.

Research Base: Small group instruction has an effect size of 0.49 (Hattie), meaning it can accelerate learning by nearly half a year's growth. When groups are formed based on specific skill needs and instruction is targeted, the effect size increases to 0.60+. This is particularly effective when combined with progress monitoring.

What Quality Implementation Looks Like

‚úÖ In the Classroom (What TO Do):

  • Groups are formed based on specific skill needs identified through data
  • Groups are flexible - students move in and out as they demonstrate mastery
  • Instruction is targeted to the specific skill gap, not just "easier" work
  • Groups meet consistently - scheduled into the week (not sporadic)
  • Teacher provides immediate, corrective feedback
  • Other students have meaningful independent work during small group time
  • Groups are temporary - 4-8 weeks for most skills
  • Progress monitoring happens within the group to track growth

‚ùå Red Flags (What NOT to Do):

  • Static groups that never change (same students all year)
  • Groups based on "high/medium/low" rather than specific skills
  • Small group gets same instruction as whole class, just repeated
  • Groups meet "when there's time" with no consistent schedule
  • Teacher works with one group while other students are off-task
  • No data used to form groups or track progress
  • Groups become permanent "ability groups" that stigmatize students
  • Small group work is just easier/less work, not targeted teaching

When to Use Small Group Instruction

Ideal Candidates for Tier 2 Small Groups:

Academic Indicators:

  • Scoring 40-70% on grade-level assessments
  • Showing gaps in specific skills (e.g., can decode but struggles with comprehension)
  • Making progress but not keeping pace with peers
  • Need more practice and support than Tier 1 provides

Behavioral/Engagement Indicators:

  • Needs more teacher proximity and attention
  • Benefits from smaller, less distracting setting
  • Needs more opportunities to respond and practice

NOT Good Candidates:

  • Students scoring above 70% (they don't need Tier 2)
  • Students scoring below 30% consistently (may need Tier 3)
  • Students who need accommodation, not different instruction (they may just need Tier 1 accommodations)

Step-by-Step Implementation

Phase 1: Forming Groups (Week 1)

Step 1: Analyze Data to Identify Skill Gaps

Don't just look at grades - identify the SPECIFIC skill.

Example - Math: - Quiz shows 8 students scored 50-65% - Analyze errors: What specific skills are they missing? * 5 students: Struggle with multi-step problems * 3 students: Struggle with negative numbers - Form 2 groups based on these specific needs Example - ELA: - Reading assessment shows 10 students below proficient - Analyze: What's the barrier? * 4 students: Decoding/fluency issues * 6 students: Comprehension (can read but don't understand) - Form 2 groups with different instructional focus

Step 2: Determine Group Size and Meeting Frequency

Group Size Guidelines:

  • 3-4 students: Intensive skill work, maximum teacher interaction
  • 5-6 students: Balanced - enough interaction, not too intensive
  • 7+ students: Too large for Tier 2 (becomes mini whole-class lesson)

Meeting Frequency:

  • Daily (20 min): Intensive skills like reading, math facts
  • 3x/week (25-30 min): Most academic skills
  • 2x/week (30 min): Minimum for measurable progress

Step 3: Schedule Small Group Time

Option A: During Whole Class Independent Work

Lesson Structure:

  • 0-15 min: Whole class mini-lesson
  • 15-45 min: Independent work begins ‚Üí Teacher pulls Group A (15 min)
  • 30-45 min: Continue independent work ‚Üí Teacher pulls Group B (15 min)
  • 45-50 min: Whole class closure

Option B: Station Rotation

Students rotate every 15 minutes:

  • Station 1: Teacher-led small group (targeted instruction)
  • Station 2: Independent practice (reinforcement)
  • Station 3: Partner work (collaborative)
  • Station 4: Technology/game (engagement)

Step 4: Plan Targeted Instruction

The instruction must be DIFFERENT and TARGETED, not just "easier."

Example - Reading Comprehension Group: - Don't: Give them an easier book and same questions - Do: Teach a specific comprehension strategy they're missing * Week 1-2: Making inferences (with explicit teaching and practice) * Week 3-4: Finding text evidence to support inferences * Week 5-6: Applying both skills to grade-level text Key Components of Small Group Lesson: 1. Quick review (2-3 min): Recap previous learning 2. Targeted teaching (8-10 min): Focus on ONE skill 3. Guided practice (8-10 min): Students practice with immediate feedback 4. Check for understanding (2-3 min): Quick assessment/exit ticket

Phase 2: Running Small Groups (Weeks 2-6)

Managing the Rest of the Class

The #1 reason small groups fail: Other students are off-task and disruptive

Solutions:

  • Teach Independence Before Starting Small Groups
    • Week 1: Practice independent work routines
    • Model: What to do if stuck (3 before me: check notes, ask partner, try)
    • Model: What to do when finished early
    • Practice: 10-minute independent work with teacher observing
  • Structure Independent Work Strategically
    • Meaningful, not busy work: Practice what they learned in Tier 1
    • Appropriate difficulty: Should be able to do independently (80%+ success rate)
    • Self-checking when possible: Answer keys, self-grading quizzes
    • Choice: "You must do #1-5, then choose 3 more from #6-12"

Small Group Lesson Structure

15-Minute Group Format: Minutes 0-2: Check-In & Review Teacher: "Show me your exit ticket from yesterday on your whiteboard." [Students hold up - teacher scans for understanding] Teacher: "Great! I see most of you remembered how to find the main idea. Today we're going to work on finding supporting details." Minutes 3-10: Targeted Teaching Teacher: "I'm going to show you my strategy for finding supporting details..." [Models with think-aloud on short passage] Teacher: "Now let's try one together..." [Guided practice - students contribute, teacher facilitates] [Do 2-3 examples together with decreasing support] Minutes 11-14: You Try Teacher: "Now you try independently. I'll be watching and helping." [Students work on passage] [Teacher circulates, gives immediate feedback to each student] Minutes 14-15: Quick Exit Ticket Teacher: "On your whiteboard, write one supporting detail from this paragraph." [Students write and hold up] Teacher: [Scans responses, notes who's ready, who needs more practice] "Excellent! Tomorrow we'll practice finding multiple supporting details."

Phase 3: Progress Monitoring & Adjusting (Ongoing)

Monitor Progress Weekly

Don't wait for unit test to see if it's working!

Example - Math Problem Solving Group: Week 1: 2/5 correct on multi-step problems Week 2: 2/5 correct (no growth - adjust instruction) Week 3: 3/5 correct (progress!) Week 4: 4/5 correct (good growth) Week 5: 4/5 correct (ready to exit? Give harder problems) Week 6: 5/5 correct on grade-level problems (EXIT GROUP)

When to Exit Students from Small Groups

Exit Criteria (Meet at least 2 of these):

  • Scoring 70%+ on grade-level tasks for 2 consecutive weeks
  • Can complete Tier 1 work independently without struggle
  • Progress monitoring shows consistent mastery
  • Teacher observation: Student no longer needs extra support
Remember: Exiting is SUCCESS, not failure!

Common Pitfalls & Solutions

‚ùå Pitfall: Groups Become Permanent "Low Group"

Problem: Same students in small group all year; groups feel like tracking

‚úÖ Solution:

  • Set clear exit criteria from the start
  • Form groups around specific, short-term skill needs
  • Celebrate when students exit
  • Change groups every 4-8 weeks based on new data

‚ùå Pitfall: Small Group Gets Same Instruction as Whole Class

Problem: Just repeating the lesson slower or with easier examples

‚úÖ Solution:

  • Identify the SPECIFIC skill they're missing
  • Pre-teach prerequisite skills they're missing
  • Use different materials, activities, or approaches
  • Focus on one micro-skill at a time

‚ùå Pitfall: Rest of Class is Off-Task/Disruptive

Problem: Teacher can't focus on small group

‚úÖ Solution:

  • Invest 1-2 weeks teaching independence routines FIRST
  • Make independent work engaging and appropriately difficult
  • Use student helpers/leaders
  • Position yourself to see the whole class

‚ùå Pitfall: No Progress Monitoring

Problem: Don't know if it's working until grades come out

‚úÖ Solution:

  • Quick weekly check (5 min): Same task, track scores
  • Keep simple graph: Week 1 (2/5), Week 2 (3/5), Week 3 (4/5)
  • If no growth after 3 weeks, change something
  • Share progress with students

Subject-Specific Strategies

Math Small Groups

Common Tier 2 Needs:

  • Multi-step problem solving
  • Fraction operations
  • Negative numbers
  • Word problem comprehension

Focus: Use manipulatives, visual models, break into explicit steps

ELA Small Groups

Common Tier 2 Needs:

  • Reading fluency
  • Reading comprehension strategies
  • Finding text evidence
  • Writing organization

Focus: Teach one strategy at a time, use shorter texts, think-alouds

Science Small Groups

Common Tier 2 Needs:

  • Scientific vocabulary
  • Reading diagrams/data
  • Lab procedures
  • Writing scientific explanations

Focus: Pre-teach vocabulary, guided data interpretation, CER scaffolding

Social Studies Small Groups

Common Tier 2 Needs:

  • Reading primary sources
  • Historical thinking (cause/effect)
  • Writing with evidence
  • Making connections across time

Focus: SIFT/SOAPS strategies, graphic organizers, sentence frames
