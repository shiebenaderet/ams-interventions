What Is It?

Gradual release of responsibility is an instructional framework where the teacher demonstrates a skill, then guides practice, and finally students practice independently. This approach follows the research-proven sequence of explicit instruction followed by scaffolded practice.

Research Base: This approach is rooted in Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development and has strong evidence for improving student learning across all content areas. Effect size: 0.57 (Hattie's meta-analysis).

What Quality Implementation Looks Like

‚úÖ In the Classroom (What TO Do):

  • Teacher thinks aloud during "I Do" (doesn't just show the answer)
  • "We Do" is true collaboration (not teacher doing it while students watch)
  • Multiple examples at each phase before moving to next phase
  • Students get immediate feedback during "We Do"
  • "You Do" starts easy and builds complexity
  • Teacher monitors during "You Do" and provides support

‚ùå Red Flags (What NOT to Do):

  • Teacher shows one example, then expects independent mastery
  • "We Do" is really just "I Do" with students copying
  • No think-aloud (students see the answer but not the thinking)
  • Moving to "You Do" when many students aren't ready
  • "You Do" is homework (no support available)

Step-by-Step Implementation

Example 1: Math Problem

I DO (5 minutes):

Teacher: "I'm going to solve this problem: 3x + 7 = 22, and I want you to watch how I think through it. First, I ask myself: What is the question asking me to find? The value of x. Next, I notice that x is being multiplied by 3, and then 7 is being added. To solve for x, I need to undo those operations in reverse order. Let me start by subtracting 7 from both sides: 3x + 7 - 7 = 22 - 7 I'm going to check: does 7 - 7 equal zero? Yes. And 22 - 7 equals 15. So now I have: 3x = 15 Now I need to undo the multiplication. I'll divide both sides by 3: 3x √∑ 3 = 15 √∑ 3 So x = 5. Last step - and this is important - I'll check my answer by plugging it back in: 3(5) + 7 = 15 + 7 = 22 ‚úì Did you notice how I worked backwards, undoing each operation?"

WE DO (10 minutes):

Teacher: "Now let's try one together: 2x - 4 = 10 Turn to your partner: What should we do first? [Students discuss] [Call on student] 'Add 4 to both sides?' Yes! Why? Let's all do that on our whiteboards... show me what you get. [Students hold up whiteboards: 2x = 14] Good! Now what? [Continue collaborating]"

Do 2-3 more problems together, gradually reducing teacher support.

YOU DO (10 minutes):

Teacher: "Now you're ready to try these on your own. Start with #1-3. I'll be walking around. [After 3 minutes] I'm seeing some common mistakes. Let's pause and talk about #2..."

Example 2: Writing (Short Answer Response)

I DO (7 minutes):

Teacher: "The question asks: Why did the colonists resist British taxation? I need to write a CER response. First, I'll write my claim - what I believe and can prove: 'The colonists resisted British taxation because they had no say in how taxes were made or used.' Now I need evidence from the text. Let me look back... [flips through reading]. Here: 'The colonists argued that taxation without representation was tyranny.' I'll write that in quotes with the page number. For my reasoning, I need to connect my evidence to my claim. Let me think... how does 'taxation without representation' connect to 'no say in taxes'? [Writes reasoning while talking]: 'This evidence supports my claim because when the colonists said taxation without representation was tyranny, they meant that Britain was making them pay taxes without giving them any voice in Parliament. They saw this as unfair and undemocratic.' Does my reasoning explain how my evidence proves my claim? Yes, I think so."

WE DO (10 minutes):

Teacher: "New question: Why did colonists protest the Stamp Act? Let's write the claim together. Turn and talk: What's your claim? [Students discuss] [Call on student] 'They thought it was unfair?' That's a start, but let's make it more specific. Let's try: 'Colonists protested the Stamp Act because it taxed everyday documents and newspapers, affecting their daily lives.' Everyone write that claim. Now, where in the text can we find evidence? I'll give you 2 minutes to find a quote... [circulate and help]"

YOU DO (15 minutes):

Teacher: "Now you'll write a full CER response to this question: Why did the Boston Tea Party happen? Use your notes and the reading. Remember the format we practiced. I'll be circulating to help."

Common Pitfalls & Solutions

‚ùå Pitfall: Moving Too Quickly

Problem: Only doing one example in "I Do" and "We Do" before independent practice

Solution: Plan for 2-3 examples at each phase. Watch for student confusion signals (furrowed brows, off-task behavior) as indicators to slow down.

‚ùå Pitfall: Silent Modeling

Problem: Teacher demonstrates but doesn't verbalize thinking

Solution: Script your think-aloud for first few times. Say things like "I'm noticing...", "This reminds me of...", "I'm confused, so I'll..."

‚ùå Pitfall: "We Do" Becomes "I Do"

Problem: Teacher does all the work while students watch

Solution: Ask students "What should I do next?" Use turn-and-talk, whiteboards, or hand signals so all students participate, not just volunteers.

‚ùå Pitfall: No Circulating During "You Do"

Problem: Teacher sits at desk while students practice

Solution: Monitor all students. Look for common errors to address with whole class. Pull small group for reteaching if needed.

Adaptations for Different Contexts

For ELL Students:

  • Provide sentence frames during "We Do"
  • Use visuals during "I Do" demonstration
  • Allow partner talk in home language during "We Do"
  • Provide vocabulary support before modeling

For Students with IEPs:

  • Extend "We Do" phase with more examples
  • Provide graphic organizers during all phases
  • Use concrete manipulatives during modeling (especially math/science)
  • Allow extended time during "You Do"

For Advanced Students:

  • Move to "You Do" more quickly if demonstrating mastery
  • During "You Do", provide extension problems or challenges
  • Allow them to create their own examples to teach peers

Monitoring Success

How do you know it's working?

  • During "We Do": 80%+ of students can correctly answer when prompted
  • During "You Do": Students can work independently with minimal questions
  • On assessments: 70%+ of students demonstrate mastery
  • Student feedback: Students can explain the steps/process

If students are struggling:

  • Return to "I Do" - model again with a new example
  • Extend "We Do" - do more guided practice
  • Break the skill into smaller steps
  • Check prerequisite skills (maybe they're missing foundational knowledge)
  • Consider moving struggling students to Tier 2 support
